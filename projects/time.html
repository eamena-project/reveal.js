Analyzing the EAMENA database with R and Python scripted routine


Thomas Huet, Kristen Hopper, Will Deadman

EAMENA Workshop 7-8th July 2022

The purpose of our presentation is to show how the use of DB can be improved by connecting it to other open source software. EAMENA is known to be a geo-web database, so the spatial information of its records is commonly used to perform analysis. To illustrate our purpose, we will focus here on its temporal dimension. This presentation is hosted online on EAMENA's GitHub repository, and can therefore be retrieved later from a simple URL. It embeds different links, interactive graphics or maps, made on open source app as the presentation itself.
In the front end, the best way to collect data before analyzing it is to "clipboard" the result of a search as a GeoJSON URL. Then the URL can be pasted into any web browser and it is converted into a GeoJSON object. Actually, JSON is the main data exchange format. From the back-end, a connector can be created with R and/or Python to connect to the Postgresql database where all the EAMENA data is stored. R and/or Python can be used in standalone files, for example hosted on GitHub (without the passwords!) or on the cloud to create all possible outputs (graphs, maps, etc., interactive or not), but also to create data-driven documents mixing computer routines and narrative text.
As a study, we will focus on the temporal dimension of EAMENA records. This is relevant because EAMENA records the different threats, simultaneous, or spread out in time, that can affect the cultural heritage. One of the simplest examples is the "date of assessment activity", i.e. the date of creation of a record. Currently, the data type of the "assessment activity date" is a date in the standard format YYYY-MM-DD (ISO 8601).

EDTF

Extended Date/Time Format (EDTF) is an iso-standard (ISO 8601-2:2019) providing a formal syntax for fuzzy dates, very precise (timestamp) dates, negative years, intervals, seasons, and a number of standardised formats
EDTF on the Library of Congress

In EAMENA, the Absolute Chronology menu uses EDTF format for the dates. EDTF is an extension of the date type format, allowing a complete record of all types of time evaluations whatever their fuzzyness.

Bam Citadel

EAMENA-0164997

Bam is situated in a desert environment on the southern edge of the Iranian high plateau. The origins of Bam can be traced back to the Achaemenid period (6th to 4th centuries BC). Its heyday was from the 7th to 11th centuries, being at the crossroads of important trade routes and known for the production of silk and cotton garments. The existence of life in the oasis was based on the underground irrigation canals, the qanāts, of which Bam has preserved some of the earliest evidence in Iran. Arg-e Bam is the most representative example of a fortified medieval town built in vernacular technique using mud layers (Chineh).

The citadel of Bam was destroyed by an earthquake in 2003, exactly on December 26, 2003, at 01h56m52s GMT. The two photographs presented here show the citadel before its destruction (left), and after its destruction (right). These photographs can be saved as information resources in EAMENA. The red boxes show how the moment of creation can be saved in EDTF format. We know that the photograph on the left was created 3 months before the earthquake, in September, but we do not yet know the exact day, so we can record it in YYYY-MM format. The date of the second photograph is unknown, but it was taken after the earthquake: the EDTF format is the concatenate of the date of the earthquake, the GMT offset (+3:30) and two leading dots indicating that the photograph was taken after the earthquake date.

Conclusion

EAMENA is a massive database that connects to open source applications. It offers all the computing facilities to semantize its content and complies with open science guidelines. EDTF is one of these international standards, and its use to record and analyze the various threats to cultural heritage will allow to model their dynamics and improve cultural heritage management. Scripted routines facilitate the management of very large volumes of data. Integrated into creative frameworks for data science - with interactive data-driven documents, and where narratives can be set up - these workflows enable powerful workflows for research or cultural heritage management. As you have seen, this presentation brings together several interactive web files generated by open source applications that can then be reused as is in other contexts (data-driven document, tutorials, social media, etc.). These days, cloud computing, online graphical interfaces for scripting languages like R and Python, or platforms like GitHub make it possible to share and co-develop entire or partial applications.
