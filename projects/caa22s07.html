According to UNESCO, cultural heritage is a "shared heritage". Our purpose today is to see how the data of this shared heritage can effectively be shared despite many obstacles. The EAMENA and MarEA projects have thus far pulled together a significant resource on cultural heritage in MENA. But it is through linking this resource, ensuring its sustainability, working towards its data analysis capabilities and its use in regional and local management and protection plans, that the true value of this resource transpires. This is where challenges however lie, in the execution and translation of this resource into a digital asset. We hope that by sharing our experiences and methods on these web-based platforms in this session, we can share our understanding on best and proven practices and learn from the different projects and research

But hopefully, CMS like Arches allow to group all these elements in a unique platform as its graph database Arches is an open source software freely available for cultural heritage organisations to help them manage cultural heritage data, devised in 2013 by Getty Conservation Institute and the World Monuments Fund. Arches uses the CIDOC CRM ontology. Open Access
EAMENA database uses Arches platform. EAMENA has been also developing national implementations of the Arches database for cultural heritage management in different countries in the MENA, including Jordan, Lebanon, and Occupied Palestinaian Territories. There are now 343,409 records in the EAMENA database, including 178,717 heritage places covering both MarEA’s maritime sites and terrestrial sites. The Digital record of archaeological sites into the database improves the inventory and future management and preservation of sites in the MENA. Our resource models or data forms enable the documentation of not only archaeological sites/heritage places, but also detailed condition assessments, environmental and geoarchaeological information. All this helps us in contextualisation heritage in the region and linking across forms to build a more rounded picture of the record we have, its condition, and threats acting upon it. We have trained More than 160 archaeological and heritage professionals in 8 countries in EAMENA methodology (Jordan, Palestine, Lebanon, Egypt, Yemen, Iraq, Libya, Tunisia). Nearly 15,000 sites have been recorded by EAMENA trainees. Over recent years EAMENA has been followed by a series of projects that now cover much of the ‘global South’ and all use the Arches platform.
Funded in 2015 by Arcadia, A Charitable Fund of Lisbet Rausing and Peter Baldwin, the Endangered Archaeology in the Middle East and North Africa (EAMENA) project is a digital documentation project of archaeological and cultural heritage sites in about 20 countries in the MENA region. It is based at the School of Archaeology of the University of Oxford, in partnership with Leceister and Durham universities in the UK. EAMENA mainly uses remote sensing but also on-the-ground methodologies for discovering and assessing threats and disturbances to cultural heritage in the MENA region. Its sister project, Maritime Endangered Archaeology (MarEA), was established in 2019 at the Universities of Southampton and Ulster, also funded by Arcadia, to rapidly and comprehensively document and assess threats to the maritime and coastal archaeology of the Middle East and North Africa. Given that the coastal zone in the MENA region is under huge threat from natural and human impacts, MarEA is addressing the critical need to document maritime cultural heritage in the region. EAMENA and the MarEA teams work closely together and use the same database in their assessment of cultural and maritime cultural heritage. The Middle East and North Africa (MENA) region is home to some of the world’s best preserved cultural heritage, including terrestrial and maritime archaeological remains, from prehistory to the Islamic period and modern times. Data collected by the EAMENA and MarEA projects show that cultural heritage sites in the MENA region are under threat from factors such as agricultural expansion, armed conflict and looting, natural/coastal erosion, construction and urban development, and climate change
