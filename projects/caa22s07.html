How do we share?

According to UNESCO, cultural heritage is a "shared heritage". The question of "what" we share is not the focus of this session: we will concentrate on immovable heritage, whether this heritage belongs to cultural heritage, natural heritage or mixed heritage. But our main question will be "how" we share this heritage, or more precisely how we share information about this heritage.

5-star LOD by T. Berners-Lee
LD is another name for the web of data. LODs are LDs with open licenses, schemas (CRMs) and metadata. To share data, the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) recommends adopting names based on HTTP URIs. With LOD we move from a document web (web 2.0) to a LD web (web 3.0). Files must be readable by a person or a machine. This statement is the basis of the semantic web: for example, citation file formats (.cff) are citation information for software (and data sets). Now, to share scientific information, such as an assessment of the condition of a heritage site, open science policies state that such information should be "accessible as much as possible and closed as much as necessary".

International standards

  • CIDOC-CRM (ISO 21127): "curated knowledge of museums"1
    • CRMarchaeo: for the excavation process based on the Harris matrix
    • CHML-3D: metadata scheme for 3D reconstruction
  • Dublin Core (ISO 15836): semantic web vocabulary used to express data and metadata
  • PeriodO: thesaurus for the definition of (pre)historical periods

So, to share data, we need to share descriptors and models. Here are some examples. CIDOC-CRM is used for the description and organisation of CH-related information. It is aligned with the Dublin Core. CRMarchaeo and CHML-3D are two extensions of CIDOC-CRM for the excavations and 3D reconstruction processes respectively. Dublin Core is a semantic web vocabulary used to express data in an RDF model. PeriodO records cultural periods using Archival Resource Key (Ark) identifiers based on the URI standard.

Cultural Heritage

Geoweb Platforms

  • ResearchSpace

ResearchSpace is a web-based platform that provides a collaborative environment for humanities, social science and CH research projects. It was developed by the British Museum. It uses, among others, the CIDOC-CRM ontology and the IIIF viewer.

Geoweb Platforms

  • Wiss-KI

Wiss-KI aims to become a reference in object-oriented document management. It is a modular extension of the Drupal content management system (CMS). It integrates the Erlangen-CRM ontology (an OWL-DL) for heavy data documentation.

Cultural Heritage

Geoweb Platforms

  • ResearchSpace
  • Wiss-KI
  • Arches

Arches is a geospatial semantic graph platform for CH management. It natively integrates Dublin Core, CIDOC-CRM, SKOS and SPARQL. Its next version (v7) will also offer internationalisation, RTL support for the user interface and import/export of multi-language data.
Arches-based projects in the Global South
Arches has been funded by the Getty Conservation Institute and the World Monuments Fund.
The Endangered Archaeology in Middle East and North Africa (EAMENA) was funded in 2015 by Arcadia, a Charitable Fund of Lisbet Rausing and Peter Baldwin, for a digital documentation of archaeological and cultural heritage sites in about 20 countries in the MENA region It uses Arches platform. EAMENA has been also developing national implementations of the Arches database for cultural heritage management in different countries in the MENA, including Jordan, Lebanon, and Occupied Palestinaian Territories. There are now more than 340,000 records in the EAMENA database, including ca 180,000 heritage places covering both Maritime Endangered Archaeology (MarEA)’s maritime sites and terrestrial sites. MaREA was established in 2019 at the Universities of Southampton and Ulster, also funded by Arcadia. The Digital record of archaeological sites into the database improves the inventory and future management and preservation of sites in the MENA. The project has trained more than 160 archaeological and heritage professionals in 8 countries in EAMENA methodology (Jordan, Palestine, Lebanon, Egypt, Yemen, Iraq, Libya, Tunisia). Nearly 15,000 sites have been recorded by EAMENA trainees. Over recent years EAMENA has been followed by a series of projects that now cover much of the ‘global South’ and all use the Arches platform. Data collected by the EAMENA and MarEA projects show that cultural heritage sites in the MENA region are under threat from factors such as agricultural expansion, armed conflict and looting, natural/coastal erosion, construction and urban development, and climate change

Publishing Platforms

  • Archaeological Data Service

The Archaeology Data Service (ADS) is hosted by the University of York. It is one of the UK's leading digital repositories of heritage data and a European reference with its participation in the ARIADNEplus infrastructure and SEADDA actions. Its aim is the long-term digital preservation of data and the promotion of good practice, including ensuring the re-use of digital data. It uses CIDOC-CRM ontologies, controlled vocabularies such as the Getty AAT and the Periodo thesaurus.
